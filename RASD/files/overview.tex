\subsection{Product Perspective}
BBP acts as a distributed system comprising a mobile app and a web app, both communicating with a central backend. The mobile app enables automatic tracking with sensor data acquisition and provides access to all system functionalities. The web app focuses on path visualization, management, and manual data insertion. Both interfaces support manual insertion of trips and bike paths. The system interacts with external entities such as weather APIs for meteorological data and map APIs for geocoding and route visualization. Public users can search and visualize bike paths, while registered users access the full feature set.

\subsubsection{Scenarios}
The following scenarios show the typical interaction with the system, highlighting how BBP reaches its main goals.

\paragraph{Scenario 1: Automatic tracking and Data Acquisition Through Sensors}\mbox{}\\
\textbf{Actors:} Alice (Registered User), Mobile App, Phone's gyroscope and accelerometer.\\
Alice with BBP's mobile app installed on her phone starts cycling.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item When Alice reaches a speed typical of cycling (between 15 km/h and 20 km/h), BBP's app detects an activity.
    \item The system starts recording Alice's route by saving GPS coordinates every 500ms.
    \item While cycling Alice hits a pothole. BBP's app registers the data acquired through the gyroscope and detects unusual vibration patterns suggesting the presence of an obstacle.
    \item The system records the GPS coordinates of the potential obstacle.
    \item Arrived at destination, Alice stops. BBP's app recognizes a prolonged stop and stops recording.
    \item The system creates a summary based on the recorded route and detected obstacles.
    \item BBP shows a summary and asks Alice to validate the acquired data (time, route, and obstacles).
    \item Alice confirms the data. The mobile app sends the information to the server and saves it in the system.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scenario 2: Manual Insertion and Path Publishing}\mbox{}\\
\textbf{Actors:} Bob (Registered User), Web App.\\
Bob decides to add a bike path he has just completed to help his local biking community.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Bob logs on the website.
    \item He selects "Add new bike path" and uses the interface to specify the streets composing the route (starting point, waypoints, destination).
    \item Bob adds a few obstacles he encountered during his path with their locations and marks the path's status as "Good".
    \item He selects to make this information public.
    \item The system validates the obstacle positions, calculates the path geometry and quality score, saves the data Bob sent and makes them available to all users, both registered and non-registered.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scenario 3: Search and Path Visualization}\mbox{}\\
\textbf{Actors:} Charlie (Non registered user), Web App.\\
Charlie needs to find the optimal bike path (with better road quality and fewer obstacles) to reach a destination.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Charlie visits the BBP's website without logging in.
    \item Through the UI he inserts the origin (his position) and the destination.
    \item The system processes the request and finds available paths, ranking them by quality score.
    \item The system displays the results. Path A has status "Fair" while Path B has status "Very good".
    \item Charlie reviews the ranked results and selects Path B due to its higher quality score.
    \item Charlie opens Path B on his device's map app.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Shared Phenomena}
As stated above the interaction between the \textit{World} and the \textit{Machine} is defined by the \textit{Shared Phenomena}, categorized by which domain they are controlled.
\begin{description}
    \item[World Controls:] \hfill \\
    \textit{Real-world events:} Bike movement, vibrations (caused by bad road quality), GPS position changes.\\
    \textit{User Input:} User inserting his credentials, user validating a Path, User searching a path, User inserts manually a bike path.\\
    \textit{External Data:} Weather conditions from weather APIs, world map received from map APIs.

    \item[Machine Controls:] \hfill \\
    \textit{Visualizations:} Map rendering, path statistics, ranked search results.\\
    \textit{User prompts:} Request to validate automatically detected obstacles.\\
    \textit{Data storage:} Trips, bike paths, obstacles, user accounts, and weather information.
\end{description}

\subsection{Product Functions}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Users managing and registration:}
    The system distinguishes between registered and non-registered users. Registered users can record trips, create bike paths, and manage their content. Path search is accessible to everyone.
    
    \item \textbf{Automatic activity tracker:}
    Mobile app monitors the user's speed, when it detects a speed typical of cycling (between 15km/h and 20 km/h) it starts recording the activity regularly saving GPS coordinates. When the user stops for a significant amount of time, the mobile app recognize the end of the cycling activity.
    \item \textbf{Obstacle and road quality inferring:}
    During automatic activity tracking, the mobile app will infer about road quality and obstacles presence through data gathered from phone sensors, such as the gyroscope and accelerometer.

    \item \textbf{Data Validation:}
    After an automatic recording is finished, the system creates a suggested review of the route. Before saving or publishing, the user can view a summary to confirm, correct, or reject the estimated data (for example, confirming a pothole). This ensures the information is accurate.

    \item \textbf{Manual Creation:}
    Registered users can manually create trips and bike paths via web or mobile app. For trips, they specify route and timings. For bike paths, they specify streets, road quality status, and obstacles.

    \item \textbf{Publication Control:}
    Users can choose to publish their bike paths (making them visible to all users) or keep them private (visible only to themselves).

    \item \textbf{Path Management:}
    Users can update or delete their bike paths, and delete their trips. Users can also report obstacles on existing public bike paths.

    \item \textbf{Search, View, and Ranking:}
    Users can search for routes by entering origin and destination. Results are displayed on a map and ranked by quality score computed from path status and obstacles.

    \item \textbf{Data Enrichment and Statistics:}
    For recorded trips, the system calculates statistics (average speed, total distance). When available, it enriches trip data with weather information (temperature, wind, conditions) from external services.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{User Characteristics}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Registered User}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Profile:} It includes occasional cyclists, commuter or athletes who often uses the bicycle.
        \item \textbf{Intent:} To use the system as a personal diary (to track their own activities) and also as a collaboration tool to help other cyclists.
        \item \textbf{Technical Skills:} Basic skills in using mobile devices and web browsers.
        \item \textbf{Hardware Equipment:} To use automatic tracking, a smartphone with GPS, accelerometer, gyroscope, and Internet connection is required. Web browser is sufficient for manual insertions and path management.
        \item \textbf{Context of Use:} They interact with the mobile app outdoors while cycling, with minimal interaction during activity for safety. Data validation occurs at rest. They also use the web app for manual path creation and management from desktop environments.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Unregistered User}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Profile:} Anyone who needs to find a bicycle path, including tourists or casual users.
        \item \textbf{Intent:} They use the system exclusively to search for routes between an origin and a destination.
        \item \textbf{Technical Skills:} The ability to navigate a website or mobile app and interact with a digital map is required.
        \item \textbf{Context of Use:} Usually accessed via web or mobile app to plan a trip.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Assumptions, dependencies and constraints}

\subsubsection{Regulatory policies}
BBP collects personal data (email, usernames, credentials) and sensitive location data (GPS coordinates) necessary for system functionality. The handling of this data must comply with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Users' passwords must be encrypted using secure algorithms. Location data must not be published unless explicitly requested by the user. The system must provide users with the ability to delete their account and associated data. Moreover, the system must include terms of service that disclaim liability for hazardous routes, placing responsibility for real-world actions solely on the user.

\subsubsection{Domain Assumptions}
The following assumptions define the operative conditions of the real world that the system considers true to guarantee the correct functioning of its functionalities.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[] \textbf{D1:} Users who want to use automatic tracking own smartphones with working GPS, accelerometer and gyroscope with sufficient calibration to detect significant movement variations.
    
    \item[] \textbf{D2:} During the usage of GPS tracking, the user's phone has enough battery to sustain a continuous use of GPS and other sensors for the entire time of the trip.
    
    \item[] \textbf{D3:} Users have a working stable internet connection to allow the communication with BBP's server.
    
    \item[] \textbf{D4:} The accuracy of the GPS signal provided by the devices is sufficient (error less than 15 meters) to enable the system to correctly associate detected or reported obstacles with the corresponding road segment.
    
    \item[] \textbf{D5:} Users provide truthful and accurate feedback during manual validation of automatically collected data and during manual entry of new routes, ensuring the reliability of the crowdsourcing system.
    
    \item[] \textbf{D6:} External services (weather APIs and map providers) maintain their standard service levels and availability.
    
    \item[] \textbf{D7:} Users' riding behavior respects typical cycling patterns such as speed and accelerations, allowing BBP's algorithms to distinguish the activity.
    
    \item[] \textbf{D8:} Historical weather conditions retrieved from external services are correct and represent conditions encountered by the user during the trip.

    \item[] \textbf{D9:} Geocoding and routing data provided by map services are accurate and up-to-date, ensuring correct path visualization and location resolution.
\end{itemize}
